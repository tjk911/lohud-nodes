The Lower Hudson Valley is getting plenty of love at this year's Oscars. Rye's Chance Kelly gives orders to Bradley Cooper in "American Sniper," Rockland's Bill Murray provides a star turn in the Wes Anderson's "The Grand Budapest Hotel" and Dobbs Ferry students in The Marching Cobras of New York provided the soundtrack to Michael Keaton's underwear-clad stroll through Times Square in "Birdman."

But there's two other nominees with local connections. Bennett Miller and Dan Futterman got nods for writing and directing "Foxcatcher," respectively. The filmmaking duo graduated from Mamaroneck High School in 1985, but they're far from the only MHS grads in Hollywood.

"We didn't teach the PACE program to necessarily make these kids go into the arts professionally," says John Fredrickson, who taught theater for the school's Performing Arts Curriculum Experience program for 27 years. "But I've got to tell you, I'm incredibly proud."

There might be something in the water, because, since 2005, Mamaroneck alum have been nominated for nine Academy Awards - in the writing and directing categories alone - though they didn't take any statues home.

The Birdcage

Release: 1996

Director: Mike Nichols

Futterman played Val Goldman in the 1996 comedy "The Birdcage," a character forced to hide the fact that he has two gay fathers, played by Robin Williams and Nathan Lane.

Futterman had only done three films before being cast in the Mike Nichols-directed film. Following Nichols' November 2014 death, Futterman told "The Hollywood Reporter" that the "Graduate" director would often have to monitor Williams' scenes from another room because he couldn't contain his giggling.

×

Crash

Release: 2004

Director: Paul Haggis

Matt Dillon's only Oscar nod came for his role as John Ryan, a racist police officer working in a diverse L.A. neighborhood, in the 2004 film "Crash."

The film boasts other Oscar darlings, including Sandra Bullock, who won Best Actress for "The Blind Side" and was nominated for "Gravity" and Don Cheadle, who was up for Best Actor for 2005's "Hotel Rwanda."

While Dillon lost out to George Clooney, "Crash" still brought home the awards for Best Film, Best Original Screenplay and Best Achievement in Editing.

×

Entourage

Release: 2015

Director: Doug Ellin

Dillon will be reprising his role as the dim-witted, hot-tempered Johnny "Drama" Chase when HBO releases the "Entourage" movie, which reunites the crew after a four year hiatus.

Having played the character for eight seasons, Dillon will surely be screaming "Victory!" when the film releases in June.

×

Moneyball

Release: 2011

Director: Bennett Miller

Miller hit a home run with 2011's "Moneyball," which scored Oscar nods for Pitt and costar Jonah Hill.

In six degrees of Lower Hudson Valley news, the "Moneyball" script was adapted for the screen by Aaron Sorkin, a Scarsdale High School grad.

This was the second time Miller collaborated with Philip Seymour Hoffman, who played Art Howe, manager of the Oakland A's in the film.

×

American Hustle

Release: 2013

Director: David O. Russell

Recruiting the help of past collaborators Bradley Cooper, Christian Bale, Jennifer Lawrence, Amy Adams, Robert De Niro and more, David O Russell gave us 2013's "American Hustle."

Russell seems to have found a winning combination, considering the film was nominated for a total of ten Academy Awards, including Russell's third directing nod and his second writing nomination. The film was also up for Best Picture, which went to "12 Years a Slave."

×

The Great Santini

Release: 1979

Director: Lewis John Carlino

In just his second feature film, "The Great Santini," O'Keefe was nominated for Best Supporting Actor for playing Ben Meechum, the son of Robert Duvall's Lt. Col. "Bull" Meechum.

O'Keefe wasn't alone, though, Duvall got a Best Acting nomination for his turn as an aggressive marine pilot.

×

Foxcatcher

Release: 2014

Director: Bennett Miller

After the success of "Moneyball," Miller followed it up with another true life sports story with "Foxcatcher," written by Dan Futterman and E. Max Frye.

Steve Carell stars as convicted murder John DuPont, and while the actor is typically seen in comedic roles, he said in an interview with Variety that, "to work with Bennett was an enormous draw. The script was excellent and intriguing and sad and absurdly funny in places and very dark."

×

The Outsiders

Release: 1983

Director: Francis Ford Coppola

Dillon's breakout role came as Dallas Winston in Francis Ford Coppola's 1983 film, "The Outsiders."

Helping to spark the Brat Pack craze of the '80s, the film also starred Emilio Estevez, Rob Lowe and Ralph Macchio.

On Coppola's days off from filming, he reportedly spent his time adapting the script to "Rumble Fish," which came out later that same year and also starred Dillon.

×

There's Something About Mary

Release: 1998

Director: Peter Farrelly, Bobby Farrelly

Proving he also has comedy chops, Matt Dillon starred as an obsessed private investigator in the 1998 comedy hit "There's Something About Mary."

If he seems especially convincing lusting after Cameron Diaz's title character in the film, that's because the two were dating in real life during filming, but broke up after production wrapped.

Somewhat unsurprisingly, "There's Something About Mary" wasn't up for any Oscars, but it was nominated for a Golden Globe for Best Motion Picture - Comedy or Musical.

×

Platoon

Release: 1986

Director: Oliver Stone

Despite his character's name being Bunny, Dillon was far from soft and cuddly in Oliver Stone's "Platoon."

His character shows a lust for violence, savagely beating a Vietnamese villager to death in one of the films more disturbing scenes.

The 1987 film was nominated for eight Academy Awards, bringing home four, including Best Picture and Best Director.

×

The Doors

Release: 1991

Director: Oliver Stone

Dillon broke on through to the other side in the 1991 Jim Morrison biopic "The Doors."

He played John Densmore, the drummer of the titular band.

×

Capote

Release: 2005

Director: Bennett Miller

Graduates of the class of '85, Bennett Miller and Dan Futterman's first major collaboration was on the 2005 film "Capote."

Miller and Futterman first met Phillip Seymour Hoffman, who plays Truman Capote in the film, as teenagers at a summer drama camp and Hoffman would go on to win Best Actor for his performance.

Miller was nominated for best director, but didn't win. The statue did come to Westchester, though, since New Rochelle's Ang Lee took home the prize for "Brokeback Mountain."

×

The Fighter

Release: 2010

Director: David O. Russell

Considering how often he casts the same actors, David O' Russell seems to be a good friend to have if you're looking for screen work.

2010's "The Fighter" was the third time Russell worked with Mark Wahlberg , following "Three Kings" in 1999 and 2004's "I Heart Huckabees."

Russell would direct Christian Bale again in "American Hustle."

"The Fighter" earned a Best Director nod for Russell, but the award went to Tom Hooper for "The King's Speech."

×

Silver Linings Playbook

Release: 2012

Director: David O. Russell

Earning David O. Russell his second directing nomination and first writing nod was 2012's "Silver Linings Playbook."

Seemingly half the cast was up for an acting Oscar, including Bradley Cooper, Robert De Niro, Jacki Weaver and Jennifer Lawrence, with Lawrence being the only winner.

Proving winning an Oscar can be a dangerous gig, Lawrence famously tripped on her dress and fell flat as she was heading to the stage to accept her award.

×

Michael Clayton

Release: 2007

Director: Tony Gilroy

When you're sharing screen time with the likes of Sydney Pollack and George Clooney, you know you're doing something right.

O'Keefe appeared in the 2007 thriller "Michael Clayton," which brought home a supporting actress statue for Tilda Swinton, as well as nominations for best actor, director, writer and film.

×

Caddyshack

Release: 1980

Director: Harold Ramis

O'Keefe wasn't the only Lower Hudson Valley regular hitting the links in "Caddyshack."

Chevy Chase has a place in Bedford and Bill Murray often calls Rockland home.

Playing caddy Danny Noonan in the 1980 comedy actually turned out to be somewhat of a prophetic role for O'Keefe, who is now an avid golfer.
